## 2. What feels authentically human (specific quotes)

- **"You sell something a little too early and watch it triple over the next two years."** — This is lived-in. The rhythm and specificity ("a little too early," "a little too long") feel like a thought Pranav has had privately, not something generated for effect.

- **"The screeners are full. The YouTube videos are confident. The WhatsApp tips are loud."** — Three short sentences with escalating social pressure. The WhatsApp detail is very Indian retail investor; an AI generating generically would have written "social media." This line feels real.

- **"It told me things that were technically true and operationally useless."** — Good phrasing. The "technically true and operationally useless" contrast is sharp and personal. Sounds like genuine frustration.

- **"Worried about what? Why? Against what benchmark? Based on which rule that *I* set?"** — The staccato interrogation here is the most human moment in the entire post. It breaks the polished surface and sounds like someone actually annoyed.

- **"I'm not sure which ones are still doing what I bought them to do."** — Unguarded, specific, slightly awkward in a good way. Feels like a real confession rather than a crafted one.

- **"The work is unmistakably mine. The accelerant is the AI."** — Short, clean, no hedging. The two-sentence paragraph lands with actual conviction.

## 3. What feels AI-written (specific quotes, with the "tell")

- **"I want to be clear about this part, because I'm going to spend the rest of this post talking about what AI *isn't* doing, which is unfair if I don't first acknowledge what it does well."** — Classic LLM transition management. No human blogger writes meta-commentary about the structure of what they're about to say. The phrase "I want to be clear about this part, because..." is a textbook LLM tell.

- **"Today's AI — Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini — is a genuinely useful thinking partner. It writes well. It explains things clearly. It can argue both sides of almost any question. For most knowledge work, it's the best tool we've ever had."** — Four consecutive short declarative sentences, each beginning with "It," all syntactically parallel. Humans don't write in this tidy rhythm. The superlative "best tool we've ever had" in sentence four is also a manufactured button-push.

- **"That gap is enormous, and almost nobody is building for it."** — Repeated almost verbatim five paragraphs later: "That gap is enormous and uncrowded." Using the same phrase twice, two sections apart, with a slight variation, is an AI pattern (seeding a callback). A human would have forgotten the first one or deliberately varied it more.

- **"I'm not trying to beat the market. I'm trying to stop being my own worst enemy."** — The "I'm not trying to X. I'm trying to Y." parallel construction is the single most recognizable LLM closing-flourish pattern. It lands like a quote card. Pranav almost certainly did not type this.

- **"One: most investing apps optimise for activity... Two: behavioural mistakes are the expensive ones... Three: I tested my own assumptions... Four: the point isn't to win arguments..."** — Numbered bold headers for a four-point argument inside a flowing section. This is document-mode thinking, not blog-mode thinking. A human would have written this as a paragraph or at most two, not a formal numbered list with bold leads.

- **"Something that knows my portfolio and the rules I've set for it. Something that reasons in plain English... Something that pushes back... Something that improves..."** — Four consecutive bullets all beginning with "Something that." Perfect anaphora. Too perfect. This is AI constructing rhetorical symmetry; a real person would have varied the structure or collapsed two of these.

- **"What it explicitly does *not* do"** followed by four bullets all beginning "It doesn't" — again, mechanically parallel bullet list. The real LLM tell here isn't any individual bullet but the *formatting choice* itself: an LLM structures information; a human would have said some version of this in two sentences mid-paragraph.

- **"The AI doesn't know more than I do about what I want to build. But it can stress-test me much faster than I could stress-test myself."** — "stress-test me" and "stress-test myself" is a verb-noun reaching construction. Nobody says "stress-test myself" in casual conversation. This is a phrase generated to sound thoughtful but doesn't come from a living speech pattern.

- **"What's the one decision you keep making that you wish a tool would help you stop making?"** — Polished reader-engagement CTA. The sentence is technically clean but has the rhythm of a product marketer, not someone genuinely curious. Real curiosity is messier: "What's the dumb thing you keep doing despite knowing better?"

- **"Where I am right now, honestly"** as a section header — The word "honestly" placed in a header is an LLM tell. It's adding performed vulnerability as a structural label, which is the opposite of actual vulnerability.

## 5. Em-dash count + hedge-word count

- **Em-dashes in draft: 21** — nearly 3x the flag threshold of 8 for ~1,800 words. This is the single most objective AI signal in the post.
- **Top 5 hedge/filler words used:**
  1. "actually" — 10 occurrences (significant overuse; 2–3 is normal)
  2. "just" — 3 occurrences
  3. "genuinely" — 3 occurrences
  4. "clearly" — 3 occurrences
  5. "honestly" — 1 occurrence (acceptable count but used as a section header, which amplifies it)
- **Paragraph-length variance: LOW** — most body paragraphs run 3–5 sentences. There are almost no one-sentence paragraphs outside the bullet lists, and no paragraph that runs long enough to feel like someone lost in a thought. Real Medium posts from individual writers tend to have more rhythm breaks: a one-liner that lands hard, a long graf that meanders and lands harder.

---

## 6. Big-picture recommendations (max 4)

1. **Cut em-dashes by two-thirds.** 21 is an almost certain AI fingerprint for any reader who pays attention. Target: 6 or fewer. Most of the em-dash uses in this draft are setting off parenthetical asides that could just be commas, or that could be cut entirely. The worst offenders are in §2 and §5.

2. **Break at least two of the bullet lists into prose.** The "Something that..." list (§4) and the "One/Two/Three/Four" section (§5) are the most AI-flavored structural choices in the post. Converting them to flowing paragraphs — even imperfect ones — will shift the voice register dramatically. A sentence fragment or a slightly run-on sentence is more trustworthy here than a perfect four-point numbered list.

3. **Cut "actually" from 10 to 3.** The word "actually" used ten times in 1,800 words signals an AI trying to add authenticity emphasis. Pick the three places where it genuinely does work ("It told me things that were technically true and operationally useless" — though "actually" doesn't appear there, that's the kind of sentence that earns it), and cut the rest.

4. **Replace the closing CTA and the "I'm not trying to X" line with something rougher.** These are the two places most likely to make a careful reader think "AI wrote this." The post earns goodwill throughout and then spends it on a polished exit. Let the last paragraph be slightly unfinished — a genuine question Pranav has, not a product-manager's reader-engagement prompt.
