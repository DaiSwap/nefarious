## 3. New issues in v0.2 (if any)

**3.1 The subtitle competes with the opening paragraph**
The subtitle reads: "There's a real gap between what AI is good at today and what an investor actually needs in the moment of deciding. Here's how I'm trying to fill it." The opening paragraph then says: "The gap between what AI is good at today and what you actually need in the moment of deciding is enormous, and almost nobody talks about it." These two sentences are saying the same thing in almost the same words. On Medium, the subtitle is visible in the feed *before* the reader clicks. By the time they read the opening paragraph, it feels like a repetition rather than a deepening. The subtitle should either (a) add a new dimension not in the opening paragraph, or (b) be more concrete/specific than the paragraph so the paragraph feels like an expansion.

**3.2 The draft metadata line is still in the file**
Line 3 reads: "*Draft v0.2 — incorporates feedback from all 5 reviewers (retail reader, editorial, skeptic, voice authenticity, Medium platform). ~1,800 words.*" This is an internal process note, not part of the published post. On Medium this will render as italicized text immediately below the title and subtitle. It will confuse a first-time reader and looks unprofessional. Must be deleted before publish.

**3.3 Series framing is still missing**
v0.1 flagged series framing as a recommended addition. v0.2 does not add it. This isn't a blocker but it is an untapped retention lever on Medium. A post explicitly numbered "Post 1 of a series" generates significantly more follows than a one-off post of equivalent quality, because readers have a reason to follow: they know more is coming and they know roughly what it will be about.

**3.4 "What's next" subhead is still the weakest subhead**
v0.1 flagged "What's next" as a zero-pull utility subhead. v0.2 has not changed it. This is the last subhead a reader sees — the one at the moment they decide whether to follow or click away. "What's next" has no tension and no forward pull. A reader skimming the post top-to-bottom (which Medium analytics show most readers do before committing) will arrive at "What's next" with no reason to read it.

## 4. Title evaluation (round 2)

- **Current**: "I'm building an AI to argue with me about my own stock portfolio."
- **Would you click in a feed?** YES — this is a genuine improvement over v0.1. "Argue with me" is emotionally specific in a way that generic AI-investing titles are not. The post is speaking to the reader's self-doubt, not to an abstract use case. The period at the end creates an unusual pause that makes it feel like a complete thought rather than clickbait. The title no longer has "Here's why" dragging it down.
- **One remaining tension**: the title says "my own stock portfolio" but the post's most original insight is about *behavioural rules* — the gap between what AI does and what you need when your rules matter. "Stock portfolio" undersells the behavioural angle. A reader looking for a standard "I built an AI stock picker" post might click, but so might readers who will bounce when they find it's about behavioural discipline rather than predictions.
- **Sharper alternative if needed**: "I built an AI to catch me breaking my own investing rules." — more specific about what the arguing is *for*, which is the post's actual differentiator. But the current title is already good enough; this is a fine-tune, not a fix.

## 5. Opening 100 words (round 2)

**Text under review:**
> "Every investor has done this dance. You sell something a little too early and watch it triple over the next two years. You hold something a little too long because admitting you were wrong feels worse than losing forty percent. The gap between what AI is good at today and what you actually need in the moment of deciding is enormous, and almost nobody talks about it.
>
> For Indian retail investors like me, the gap is wider."

**Hook strong enough?** YES — this is a significant improvement from v0.1. The three elements now work in the right order: universal behaviour (sentence 1) → emotional specificity (sentences 2–3) → thesis (sentence 4) → geographic narrowing (paragraph 2). A global Medium reader hits the thesis before they're asked to self-identify as Indian.

**One remaining note**: "almost nobody talks about it" is slightly self-congratulatory. On Medium this kind of claim ("I'm talking about something nobody talks about") can land as arrogant to a sceptical reader. The emotional experience in sentences 2–3 is already doing the hook work — the "almost nobody" claim is not needed and could be tightened out. Not a blocker; a polish note.
