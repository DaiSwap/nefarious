## §1 — Voice verdict

**NEEDS-VOICE-WORK**

Not AI-dominant — the plan has real bones and the Blog #1 baseline proves the writer can do this authentically. But the plan telegraphs at least 6 structural patterns that, if carried forward unchallenged into the draft, will produce an AI-sounding post. The risk is not that the writing will obviously sound like a chatbot. The risk is subtler: it will sound like a sharp human who has been editing with AI long enough that some of AI's preferred rhythms have crept into what feels natural. That is the harder tell to catch, and it is the one this review exists for.

The specific patterns to fix before drafting: the "It's not X. It's Y." parallel constructions (two instances), a textbook AI hook cadence in the 6-sentence opening, the performed humility tone that is distinguishable from real learning-voice, and one closing line that reads like a rehearsed Medium outro. These are fixable. None of them require restructuring the post. They require deliberate phrase-level choices during drafting.

## §2 — Per-question voice analysis (1–7)

### Q1 — The 6-sentence hook: second-person setup → first-person flip

**The draft hook:**
> "If you read Indian investing books or watch the popular YouTube channels, you've probably heard about the Piotroski F-Score."

**Verdict: AI-tell. Medium severity.**

This is the textbook AI opening move for a "debunking" post. The structure is: (a) acknowledge the reader's world, (b) validate their prior knowledge, (c) then flip. AI uses this because it is technically sound rhetorical structure — it avoids alienating the reader by assuming shared ground before pivoting. Humans use it too, but almost never as the very first sentence. Real writers who know their readers don't begin with a reassurance that the reader has heard of the thing. They assume it and start somewhere more charged.

The secondary tell is the phrase "you've probably heard." It is hedge-qualified, inclusive, and polite. Correct in all technical senses. That is the problem. Nobody writing from genuine memory says "you've probably heard about the Piotroski F-Score." They say something more jagged because they are inside the experience, not narrating from outside it.

**Additional problem**: The hook announces that the F-Score failed before the reader has any reason to care. The structure gives the conclusion away in sentence 4 ("The checklist is giving the opposite of the right answer"). The journey from setup to reveal is six sentences. That is brisk, but the information architecture is backwards for an authentic learner's voice — a real person building a system stumbles into the result; they don't open by foregrounding it.

**The "still learning" framing makes this worse, not better.** If the writer is genuinely still learning and sharing something that surprised them, the hook should carry the texture of surprise — not the shape of a lesson already packaged for delivery.

### Q2 — The "Score 5 → +355% / Score 8 → +34%" framing

**The plan language:**
> "Score 8 or 9, and the company is supposedly a strong buy. Score 3 or below, and it's a sell."
> (combined with hook) "The score said: avoid this. The stock then went up 355% over two years. I tested it at the peak... The score said: strong buy. The stock then matched the market — boring."

**Verdict: Partially AI-tell. Low-medium severity.**

The numbers themselves are legitimate and carry real weight. The issue is the symmetry of presentation. "355% / matched the market" is a rhetorically clean opposition: one huge number vs one flat descriptor. That clean opposition is not wrong, but a real person telling this story to a friend would not have assembled those two data points into such tidy antithesis on their first pass. They would have led with the astonishing number and circled back awkwardly to the second: "then I tested it a year later and it basically just... sat there. Matched the index or something."

The secondary issue is "matched the market — boring." The word "boring" is doing authorial voice work, which is fine, but it is a precise word dropped in a precise slot. It reads as a placed punchline rather than a natural aside. AI is very good at placed punchlines.

The "+34%" figure also flattens too cleanly against "+355%." A real data-driven person describing a 34% return in 24 months (which is actually decent) might complicate it slightly rather than reducing it to "just matched the market." That complication would be more human.

---

### Q3 — §4 framing: "It's not that Piotroski is wrong. It's that he never said this was for cyclicals."

**Verdict: AI-tell. High severity. Fix before drafting.**

This is the single most AI-sounding sentence in the plan. The "It's not X. It's Y." construction is one of the clearest AI rhythmic tells because AI uses it constantly as a clarification device — it is structurally fair, it protects against misreading, and it sets up a nuanced distinction. All good reasons for AI to reach for it. Real writers reach for it too, but much less frequently, and almost never in two instances close together in the same piece (this plan has two, see Q4).

The specific version here has an additional tell: it is a defense of Piotroski embedded inside a critique of Piotroski. AI systems are trained to be fair to cited sources. A human writer who has actually been burned by following the F-Score on a cyclical stock would be less instinctively protective of Piotroski. Not wrong about the protection — Piotroski does not deserve blame — but the framing would be rougher, less lawyerly, more personal. Something like: the paper never claimed this. We just assumed it did.

---

### Q4 — §6 framing: "It's not perfect. But it's better than the textbook version for this class of stock."

**Verdict: AI-tell. High severity. Fix before drafting.**

Second instance of "It's not X. But Y." in the same post plan. This one is even cleaner, which makes it more suspect. "It's not perfect. But it's better" is a sentence pair that AI generates with near-automatic regularity when describing an improved but imperfect solution. It is grammatically correct, rhetorically balanced, appropriately humble, and completely devoid of the writer's actual voice.

A human who has been working with an adapted 5-year-average version of the F-Score and found it more useful would not package their experience as "it's not perfect but it's better." They would say something messier and more specific: what they actually saw change, what it got wrong the first time they tried it, what made them decide to keep using it despite the imperfection. The sentence in the plan closes off that detail before it can appear.

Two instances of this pattern in one 2000-word post is too many. The draft needs to rewrite both before any other voice work happens.

---

### Q5 — Closing §7: "If you want to follow along: [link to blog #1] explains what I'm building..."

**Verdict: Borderline. Low severity but worth attention.**

The Medium-CTA close is not wrong. Blog #1's closing — "If you'd like to follow this build, I'll write more as it goes" — is clearly the ancestor, and that line works because it is understated and does not perform warmth. The §7 draft version in this plan adds a "If you want to follow along:" setup that is one rung more formulaic. The colon-then-list structure feels like a writer who has started thinking in Medium CTAs rather than writing an ending that earns the link.

The deeper problem: the closing line as planned is a CTA that arrives before the post has emotionally resolved. The §7 plan is about what the test taught the writer about "imported math" — that is a genuinely interesting intellectual moment. The CTA is currently attached to the tail of that reflection before the reflection has a chance to land. The emotional close should come after the idea closes, not during.

This is a drafting concern more than a plan concern, but raising it here because the plan specifies the closing line verbatim and the pattern will transfer.

---

### Q6 — "This is what I'm doing for the whole system I'm building."

**Verdict: AI-tell. Medium severity.**

This sentence reads rehearsed because it is doing two jobs simultaneously — it closes the local point (the adapted F-Score) AND gestures toward the larger project — and it does both with a completeness that sounds like a summary rather than a thought. Real writers either close the local point and let the gesture toward the larger project come more casually, or they make the bridge explicit and messy: "That's become the pattern for everything else I'm doing here, honestly." or "That's basically the shape of this whole thing I'm building — you find where the math breaks, you fix it, you keep going."

"This is what I'm doing for the whole system I'm building" has the shape of a thesis statement that has been cleaned up until it became smooth. The smoothness is the tell.

---

### Q7 — The "still learning" tone: authentic vs performed humility

**Verdict: Mixed. The plan intention is correct. The phrase-level execution will drift performative unless managed.**

The locked tone ("still learning, not done") is the right call for this post. The authentic version of that voice exists in Blog #1 and it is genuinely good — the Blog #1 line "A month in, I'm not at a finished product. I'm at: 'I've learned, by testing, that a lot of standard investing math doesn't transfer cleanly to Indian companies'" is exactly right. That is a real admission with a specific shape: it names where the writer is, names what they learned, and offers no false modesty. It does not say "I might be completely wrong about all of this." It says: here is what I found.

The plan's tone guidance drifts from that into phrases that read as performed hedging:

**Phrases in the plan that already read as performed humility (flag for draft):**

- `"from what I'm seeing"` — acceptable but weakens every sentence it touches. Use sparingly, not as a blanket qualifier across §3.
- `"I might be wrong about this"` — this is the most dangerous one. A learner-voice that genuinely means "I might be wrong" would say something more specific: "I only tested one stock." The general "I might be wrong" is epistemically hollow — of course you might be wrong, everyone might be wrong about everything. The specific version is both more honest and more confident.
- `"the data I have so far suggests"` — technically fine, not a tell by itself. Becomes a tell when it is used as a prefix to every empirical claim. One instance in the post is authentic. Three or more is a hedging tic.
- `"would love feedback from anyone who's tested this on more stocks"` — this is the most performative line in the plan. It is Community-Building Language that an AI would naturally generate as a "engagement prompt." A real writer asking for feedback names their specific uncertainty: "I'm genuinely not sure if this pattern holds for other commodity producers — if you've run F-Scores on fertilizer stocks or oil refiners, I'd be curious what you saw." That is a real question. "Would love feedback from anyone" is not.

The distinction between genuine learning-voice and performed hedging: genuine learning-voice has a specific shape of uncertainty. It knows what it found (the data points), it knows the limits of what it found (one stock, four dates), and it asks specific questions. Performed humility is general and gestural. It hedges everything uniformly, which paradoxically reads as less honest than a sharper claim made with explicit caveats.

## §3 — Phrase-by-phrase rewrites

The following are specific rewrites of the plan's draft sentences, with the AI pattern labeled and a human-cadence alternative.

---

**Hook — sentence 1**

*Current plan draft:*
> "If you read Indian investing books or watch the popular YouTube channels, you've probably heard about the Piotroski F-Score."

*AI pattern:* Second-person reassurance opening. Epistemic hedge ("you've probably"). Reader assumed ignorant until validated.

*Human alternatives (three options — pick one):*

Option A (start in the middle of the action):
> "I was six months into building my investing system when I ran a check on a commodity stock I'd been watching. The Piotroski F-Score said: don't touch it. The stock then went up 355% over the next two years."

Option B (lead with the number, explain the checklist second):
> "The checklist told me to avoid the stock in March 2020. It went up 355% from there. When I ran the same checklist a year later, it said strong buy. The stock matched the index and went nowhere. Then I looked at what the checklist was actually measuring."

Option C (closer to the plan's structure but cut the epistemic hedge):
> "The Piotroski F-Score is a 9-point checklist that a lot of Indian retail investors use. Including me, until last year. Then I tested it on a cyclical stock at four different points in its cycle, and the results were strange enough that I had to write them down."

---

**Hook — "The stock then matched the market — boring."**

*Current plan draft:* "The stock then matched the market — boring."

*AI pattern:* Placed punchline. "boring" is too precise and too comfortable — it lands without texture.

*Human rewrite:*
> "The stock just sat there. Two years, roughly flat, maybe up with the market."

Or:
> "The stock basically matched the Nifty. Which, after 355%, felt like a lot of nothing."

---

**§4 — "It's not that Piotroski is wrong. It's that he never said this was for cyclicals."**

*AI pattern:* Classic parallel "It's not X. It's Y." construction. Too clean, too lawyerly, protects the source too efficiently.

*Human rewrite (option A — rougher):*
> "Piotroski's original paper was about US value stocks in the late 1990s. He didn't say anything about cyclicals. We just assumed the checklist would generalise. That assumption is where the problem is."

*Human rewrite (option B — more personal):*
> "The checklist isn't broken. It's being used somewhere its designer never intended it to go. The original paper was on US value stocks. Applying it to Indian commodity producers — that was us, not him."

*Human rewrite (option C — punchy):*
> "Piotroski didn't say the F-Score works on cyclicals. We assumed it did. That assumption is what the data broke for me."

---

**§6 — "It's not perfect. But it's better than the textbook version for this class of stock."**

*AI pattern:* Second instance of "It's not X. But Y." Balanced sentence pair. Authorial closure without substance.

*Human rewrite (option A — name the imperfection):*
> "The 5-year average version has its own problems — if a company went through a one-time restructuring, the average absorbs it badly. But for a normal cyclical, it stops the signal from screaming BUY at exactly the wrong moment. That's enough for me to use it."

*Human rewrite (option B — less polished):*
> "It's a rougher version of the same idea. I'm not saying it's right. I'm saying it's less wrong for this specific thing."

*Human rewrite (option C — direct):*
> "The 5-year version doesn't solve everything. But on the tests I've run, it doesn't invert at cycle turning points the way the standard version does. That's the specific thing I was trying to fix."

---

**§7 — "This is what I'm doing for the whole system I'm building."**

*AI pattern:* Rehearsed summary gesture. Thesis-statement texture. Too complete.

*Human rewrite (option A):*
> "That's kind of become the pattern for this whole project — find where the imported math breaks, figure out why, patch it or flag it. Repeat."

*Human rewrite (option B — shorter):*
> "I've been doing this with every formula I've tried to use. The textbook version, then the version that actually works on Indian data."

*Human rewrite (option C — most casual):*
> "Honestly, this is what most of what I've been building looks like. Take a formula. Test it on real Indian stocks. Find where it breaks. Fix it. Write it down."

---

**Closing CTA — "If you want to follow along: [Blog #1] explains what I'm building..."**

*AI pattern:* Medium-CTA formula with colon-then-list structure. Arrives before emotional close.

*Human rewrite:*
Write the emotional close of §7 first — the "what this taught me about imported math" reflection — let it land for a full paragraph. Then, after the idea has closed, link informally:

> "If you've tested the F-Score on other cyclicals and seen different results, I'd genuinely like to know — [first post here] has more context on what I'm building and why."

Or more casually:
> "The first post explains the overall system I'm building if you want the bigger picture."

The key is: the link should feel incidental to a thought that is already complete, not like the engineered payoff of a CTA structure.

---

**"Would love feedback from anyone who's tested this on more stocks"**

*AI pattern:* Community-building engagement prompt. General rather than specific.

*Human rewrite:*
> "I ran this on one stock at four dates. If you've computed F-Scores on fertilizer stocks, steel peers, or oil refiners, I'm curious whether the inversion pattern holds there too — or whether it's specific to the way Tata Steel's cycle works."

(Note: "Tata Steel" would be anonymized as "Stock X" in the published version, but the specificity of the ask is the point — name the actual stocks/sectors you want feedback on.)

## §4 — What rings authentically human

Not everything in this plan reads as AI. Several elements are genuinely good and should be protected in drafting.

**"I computed the F-Score by hand from the company's annual reports. (No shortcuts. Real numbers.)"**
This is the best sentence in the plan. The parenthetical lands as a real aside from someone who has actually done this and knows that readers are skeptical. Keep the "(No shortcuts. Real numbers.)" verbatim or very close to it. It is specific, slightly defensive in the right way, and does not perform humility — it asserts something the writer earned.

**"That second part is the kicker."**
Short, unpolished, conversational. This is Blog #1 energy. Keep it.

**The §5b "Quick checklist" structure (metals / oil / housing / auto / power / ancillaries)**
The list itself reads as written by someone who has thought this through on real Indian market data. It is concrete, it is not exhaustive in a manicured way, and the categories are recognisable to the target reader. This section will not need voice fixes — it reads like field notes.

**"If a reader thinks I'm trashing Piotroski — that's a fail."**
This is in the Risks section, not the post itself, but the directness here is the right voice for the whole post. The draft should write toward that bluntness, not away from it.

**Blog #1's "A month in, I'm not at a finished product. I'm at: 'I've learned, by testing...'"**
This is the benchmark for the "still learning" tone. When writing §3's disclosure and caveats, aim for that sentence's specificity. It knows exactly what it has and exactly what it doesn't have. It doesn't perform modesty — it reports a state.

**The two-stock comparison structure (Stock X vs Stock Y)**
The conceptual architecture here is strong and inherently shows its work. A post that makes a claim and then immediately shows the counter-case is structurally honest in a way that does not require hedging language to signal. If the §5 contrast section is written cleanly and the data holds, it will land with more credibility than a dozen "I might be wrong about this" qualifications.
