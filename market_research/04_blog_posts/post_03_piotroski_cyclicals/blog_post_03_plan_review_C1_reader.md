### Q1. The hook (6-sentence lede): does it pull you in despite your skepticism?

**Score: 7/10**

My honest first-pass: I clicked because the title is punchy and I do use the F-Score on Tickertape. By sentence four — "The stock then went up 355% over two years" — you have my attention. That number is real and specific enough that I don't immediately dismiss it.

But there is a problem on sentence six: "The checklist is giving the opposite of the right answer for this kind of stock." That sentence, coming at the end of the hook before I have seen a single piece of supporting evidence, reads exactly like what I expect from an "AI guy" who ran some numbers on a weekend and decided a Nobel-adjacent framework is junk. My guard goes up immediately after it.

The fix is subtle but important. Replace that sentence with something that signals curiosity rather than conclusion: "I'm not sure if this is a pattern or a coincidence — but the inversion was big enough that I couldn't ignore it." That keeps me in the tent instead of making me pre-load my counterargument.

The other six sentences are clean. The voice is close enough to Blog #1 that it does not feel like a different writer. The framing — "I tested it, here's what surprised me" — is right.

The hook earns its 7 because +355% is a number I recognise from Tata Steel's COVID-to-commodity-supercycle run. Even anonymised, any NIFTY-100 investor who was around in 2020–2022 knows exactly which metal stock you are talking about. That recognition makes it feel real.

### Q2. §1 (what the F-Score is): do you learn anything new, or does it feel patronizing?

**Score: 6/10**

This is the hardest section to pitch and the plan is at about two-thirds of the right calibration.

The problem is the asymmetry in the audience. Half of your readers found you through a Screener.in search for "F-Score > 7 NIFTY 100" and have been using this score for three years. They know the nine questions. Reading "Is the company making more money this year than last?" feels like being told what a P/E ratio is.

The other half heard Saurabh Mukherjea say "F-Score 8 or 9 is very strong" in a YouTube video, nodded, and moved on. They do not know how it is constructed. For them, the section is genuinely useful.

The plan as written pitches entirely to the second half. The diagram (9-question flowchart in 3 subgraphs) is aimed at people who need the map, not at people who already have it memorised.

There is a phrase in §4 that would actually solve this if moved into §1: "he never said this was for cyclicals." That line is new information for almost everyone. Most Indian investors who use the F-Score have no idea that the original Piotroski paper was specifically a value-stock screen on US stocks from 1996. The paper is cited in books but not summarised. If §1 opens with "here is the one thing about the F-Score that almost no Indian book mentions" and leads to that detail, it stops feeling patronizing and starts feeling like a real insight.

Recommendation: cut the "Is the company making more money" plain-English list by half. Replace the space with one paragraph on the origin and scope of the original paper — US value stocks, 1996, not tested on cyclicals, not intended for cyclicals. That positions the reader for §4 without spoiling it.

### Q3. §3 (Stock X table — F=5 +355% / F=8 +34%): convincing or immediately "small sample"?

**Score: 5/10**

I am going to be honest: my first reaction to a 4-row table is "4 dates on 1 stock is not a pattern, it's a cherry-pick."

That reaction is not irrational. It is the same reaction any person who has been burned by confirmation bias — including me — will have. The plan acknowledges this risk in the "Risks" section: "One-stock evidence: acknowledge this in §3." But the proposed acknowledgment ("I tested it on one stock at 4 dates. That's not statistical proof. But the pattern lines up with how cyclicals work in theory") is not strong enough.

Here is my specific objection: the four dates are not independent. March 2020, March 2021, March 2022, March 2024 are all readings on the same underlying cycle of the same stock. The correlation between F-Score and cycle position is structural — once you have confirmed it at one date, the other three dates are mechanically predictable if the hypothesis holds. The reader who thinks about this for twenty seconds will notice that you have one degree of freedom, not four.

What would actually move me: name two or three other cyclical stocks and say "I checked JSPL in March 2020 and saw the same inversion" or "SAIL at the same date showed the same pattern." You do not need full tables for each — one sentence per additional stock is enough. That changes the claim from "I found this on one stock" to "I found this on every steel and metal stock I looked at over the same window," which is a qualitatively different level of evidence.

The table itself is well-designed. The "What the score said" column next to "What the stock did" is the right visual contrast. The writing in that column is crisp. If the sample concern is addressed, the table becomes very effective.

Also: the March 2024 row with "(incomplete window, lagging)" is a mistake. Do not include it in the table. It makes the dataset look incomplete and gives sceptics a free shot. Leave 2024 for a single sentence in §6: "I have begun the same check on the current cycle position; I'll update when I have a full two-year forward window."

### Q4. §4 (the deeper "why"): respectful or condescending to the community?

**Score: 7/10**

The line "The Indian investing community quietly extended it to all stocks" is the post's most sensitive sentence. How it lands will determine whether a large portion of your readers feel seen or feel scolded.

My read: it is close to respectful but needs one more sentence of cushion before it.

The problem is the word "quietly." It implies the community did something they knew was wrong and did not say so out loud. That is probably not what happened. What happened is more innocent: a book cited the F-Score without explaining the scope caveat, a YouTuber ran with it, Screener.in added it to their filters, and now everyone uses it on everything because it has worked well enough that no one tested the edge case. That is not "quiet extension" — that is normal diffusion of a financial idea.

Suggested reframe: "Piotroski never tested it on cyclicals. Neither did most of the books that brought it to Indian investors — not because they were careless, but because cyclicals are a genuinely tricky edge case that only shows up when you test at the bottom of a cycle." This gives the community credit for reasonable behaviour rather than implying they looked the other way.

The mechanistic explanation — "by the time the F-Score is telling you things are improving, the smart money has already bid up the stock" — is correct and lands well. The cycle-position diagram (Diagram 3, sine-wave loop) will help significantly here. The flowchart as drafted (four nodes in a loop, color-coded) is the right approach.

One thing the plan does not include that would make §4 significantly stronger: a single number showing when the stock price actually moved versus when the F-Score moved. Something like: "Stock X's price started moving in October 2020. The F-Score only crossed 7 in March 2021 — five months later." That concretises the "lag" argument without requiring any additional data the author probably does not already have.

### Q5. §5 (Stock Y non-cyclical contrast): reinforce or feel like a half-defence?

**Score: 6/10**

I have mixed feelings about this section.

On one hand, showing the F-Score working on a non-cyclical is the right structural move. It prevents the post from reading as "the F-Score is broken" and instead focuses the argument on "the F-Score breaks for this specific class of stock." That is an important distinction.

On the other hand, the table as planned uses illustrative numbers with a note that says "(Specific numbers to be confirmed during drafting; pattern is the point — for stable businesses, the F-Score moves WITH the stock, not against it)." If those numbers are illustrative and the reader who knows Asian Paints well reads them and they do not match what they can find on Screener.in, the entire post loses credibility. Asian Paints' pre-2021 numbers are publicly available and any serious investor can check them in five minutes.

My strong recommendation: use real numbers or cut Stock Y to a shorter mention. The plan gives Stock Y about the same real estate as Stock X. That balance is wrong given the evidence asymmetry. Stock X has specific hand-computed data at four specific dates with real forward returns. Stock Y, as planned, has illustrative numbers. The reader will sense that asymmetry and it will undermine Stock X's data.

If the real Asian Paints numbers support the narrative: use them. If they are messy (which the plan acknowledges is possible): one paragraph in prose saying "I ran the same check on a large consumer-goods stock and the F-Score tracked the stock's direction much more closely" is enough. Do not build a second table for data you are not confident in.

### Q6. §6 (what I do instead — 5-year-average): actionable for you?

**Score: 4/10**

This is where the post loses me most.

"I switched to a 5-year-average version of the same questions. Instead of 'is profit higher than last year', I now ask 'is profit higher than the 5-year average'."

My immediate thought: that sounds right in principle. My second thought: how would I actually do this on Screener.in or Tickertape?

The answer is that I cannot. Screener.in lets me write custom formulas, but computing a 5-year average profit baseline and then comparing current profit to that average is not a pre-built screen. It requires either knowing Screener.in's formula syntax well enough to build it from scratch, or using the Screener.in API, or exporting to Excel. None of these are things most retail investors will do.

This is the "you need a programmer to do this" problem the prompt flagged. The plan does not address it.

Two options:

Option A: Be explicit that this is a programmatic fix, and frame it honestly: "I built this into my own system, so I cannot give you a one-click Screener filter. But I can tell you what to look for manually: if you are looking at a metal or fertiliser stock, scroll back five years on Screener.in and eyeball whether this year's margins are high or low relative to that history. If they are near a five-year high, be cautious even if the F-Score looks strong."

Option B: Give a proxy that is actionable with existing tools. "When I look at a cyclical stock, I check P/B ratio alongside the F-Score. At a cyclical trough, both will look bad. At a cyclical peak, both will look great. If P/B is near a 5-year high and the F-Score is above 7, that combination has historically been the worst time to enter."

Option A is more honest. Option B is more actionable. Either is better than the current plan which leaves the reader with a correct idea they cannot execute.

Also: the warning label ("this score may be reading the cycle, not the company's quality") is a good idea and should be kept.

### Q7. The "still learning" tone: does it determine whether I accept the finding?

**Score: 8/10**

Yes, absolutely. And the plan's commitment to this tone is its single strongest decision.

If this post were written in a declarative register — "The F-Score is broken for cyclicals; here's the data; here's my fix" — I would write it off immediately. I have seen that post written by people who run backtests on 500 stocks and still get it wrong. I have no reason to trust a single person's system analysis over Piotroski's original paper.

But "I found something that surprised me; I want to hear if others have seen the same" is a completely different invitation. It does not ask me to abandon my belief in the F-Score. It asks me to help test a hypothesis. That is something I might actually do.

The specific sentence-level cues in the plan ("from what I'm seeing", "I might be wrong about this", "the data I have so far suggests") are right. The closing line linking back to Blog #1 is right — it signals continuity and a genuine project rather than a one-off contrarian take.

The one risk: the "still learning" framing can tip into excessive hedging if overused. If every other paragraph ends with a disclaimer, it starts to read as insecurity rather than intellectual honesty. The plan should aim for two or three explicit "I might be wrong" moments per post, not ten.

### Q8. The locked disclosure ("not peer-reviewed, one stock at 4 dates"): trust or undermine?

**Score: 8/10**

This is the right call and it should be placed early — §1 or immediately before the table in §3, not at the end.

The reason for early placement: if the disclosure comes after I have already read the table and partially updated my beliefs, I will feel slightly manipulated. "Why didn't you tell me this upfront?" But if it comes before the data, it reframes my reading posture. I go into the table knowing this is a directional signal, not a proof. That is a much more stable way to present uncertain data.

One suggestion on the language: "not peer-reviewed" is the right phrase, but add a sentence explaining what that means in practice. Many retail investors vaguely associate "peer-reviewed" with "academic journal" without knowing what the process involves. Something like: "I haven't tested this on hundreds of stocks, and no one else has checked my numbers — so treat this as a starting hypothesis, not a conclusion" is clearer and more personal.

The disclosure builds trust specifically because it is proactive. Any reader who is inclined to be sceptical — and this audience will be — will respect an author who names the limitations before they have to ask.

### Q10. Diagrams 4–5: will I understand them on Medium?

**Score: 6/10**

Two specific concerns.

First: Mermaid diagrams on Medium are notoriously inconsistent. Some publications render them; many do not. Medium's native editor does not support Mermaid natively — it typically renders as a code block. This means the author must export the diagrams as images (PNG/SVG) and embed them manually. The plan does not address this. If the author publishes and all five diagrams appear as grey code blocks, the post fails visually.

Second: the Gantt chart for Diagram 2 is the wrong choice for this data. Gantt charts communicate duration and sequence. What this diagram needs to communicate is inversion — specifically, the mismatch between F-Score signal direction and stock price direction. A Gantt chart does not show direction; it shows time. The flowchart alternative mentioned in the plan ("could use a flowchart with up/down arrows instead") is much better. Use it.

Diagram 1 (the 9-question breakdown in 3 subgraphs) is the right choice for that section and will render cleanly as a flowchart.

Diagram 3 (the cycle loop with 4 nodes) is simple enough to render correctly on most platforms and is the most important diagram in the post — it is the mechanism diagram. Keep it exactly as designed.

Diagram 4 (Stock Y contrast, same style as Diagram 2) depends entirely on whether the flowchart approach replaces the Gantt. If yes, it works. If the Gantt stays, both Diagrams 2 and 4 will underperform.

Diagram 5 (the "where F-Score works vs where it breaks" two-column split) is a good idea but should be the last to build. If the post hits the length ceiling, this is the cut.

## §3 — What WILL Push Me to Override and Stay Loyal to the F-Score

These are the arguments I will fall back on if the post does not convincingly answer them:

**1. Sample size.** One stock at four dates, all drawn from the same cycle, is not enough to change a framework I have used on fifteen stocks and seen work. If you want to move me, you need either more stocks or a theoretical argument strong enough to stand without data. The plan tries to offer both but delivers neither cleanly — the data is thin and the theory ("cyclicals are mean-reverting by definition") is stated but not developed. I need one or the other to be airtight.

**2. Selection bias.** You chose to test at March 2020. Why? Because it was the trough. Any metric would have looked weak at a COVID trough. If you had tested the F-Score at March 2019 (a non-COVID trough in the steel cycle), would you have seen the same inversion? If no, then March 2020 is a unique event, not a structural problem with the F-Score.

**3. It works for me.** I have screened for high F-Score stocks on Tickertape and bought three of them over the past two years. Two outperformed. One was flat. That is a 2-for-3 hit rate. Your argument is asking me to conclude that those two wins were luck and the flat one was the F-Score accurately reading a cyclical. But I did not buy cyclicals — I specifically looked for FMCG and pharma names. So the F-Score may have been working fine for the stocks I applied it to, and your post may simply not apply to my actual usage pattern.

This third objection is the hardest for the post to address because it requires me to self-identify as "someone who uses the F-Score on cyclicals." If I do not do that, the post is not for me. The §5b sector checklist is the only thing that will force me to confront whether I have ever done this by accident.

## §4 — What WILL Convince Me to Update My View

These are the specific things that would actually shift my beliefs:

**1. A second metal stock showing the same inversion in the same window.** If you say "I ran the same check on JSPL at March 2020 and saw F=4 and +280% forward," I am 60% convinced. If you add SAIL, I am 80% convinced. You do not need tables for each — one sentence per stock is enough. This single change has the highest return on effort of any fix in the plan.

**2. The specific month when the stock price started moving versus when the F-Score moved.** If the stock price started recovering in Q3 FY21 and the F-Score only crossed 7 in March 2021, that temporal gap is the entire argument. One sentence with two specific dates is more convincing than four rows in a table.

**3. The §5b sector checklist explicitly naming something I own.** If I own an auto-ancillary or a fertiliser stock and the post names those sectors as "places where the F-Score may be reading the cycle, not the company," I will go and check my own holding. That check — whether or not it confirms your thesis — is the highest-quality engagement outcome for this post.

**4. The "still learning" framing landing as genuine rather than defensive.** If the humility sounds performative, it backfires — it reads as the author pre-empting criticism without actually being uncertain. The test is whether the closing paragraph gives me something to do: "If you've tested the F-Score on a metal stock at the trough of the cycle, I'd genuinely like to know what you found." An open question is more convincing than a hedge.

**5. The author admits one case where the inversion did NOT hold.** The most trust-building move in the entire post is a single sentence like: "I looked at Hindalco at the March 2020 trough and the F-Score was 6 — the inversion was there but smaller. I'm not sure if that's because Hindalco's business mix is different or because I'm missing something." Acknowledging a messy data point does more for credibility than a clean four-row table does.
